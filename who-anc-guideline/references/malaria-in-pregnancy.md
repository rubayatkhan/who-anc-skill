# Malaria in Pregnancy — Prevention and Treatment

## Overview

Malaria in pregnancy causes maternal anemia, low birth weight, preterm birth, and increased maternal/neonatal mortality. In endemic areas, prevention is integrated into routine ANC through a three-pronged approach: IPTp-SP, ITNs, and prompt case management.

## IPTp-SP (Intermittent Preventive Treatment with Sulfadoxine-Pyrimethamine)

### Eligibility
- **Setting**: Moderate-to-high malaria transmission areas (most of sub-Saharan Africa)
- **GA**: ≥ 13 weeks (second trimester onward)
- **HIV status**: Eligible UNLESS on daily cotrimoxazole prophylaxis (drug interaction)

### Dosing
- **Drug**: Sulfadoxine 500mg / Pyrimethamine 25mg (3 tablets as single dose)
- **Administration**: Directly Observed Therapy (DOT) — must be taken at the clinic under observation
- **Frequency**: At each scheduled ANC contact, at least 1 month apart
- **Minimum doses**: 3 during pregnancy (WHO recommends starting early and giving at every contact)

### Schedule Integrated with ANC Contacts

| Contact | GA (weeks) | IPTp-SP Dose | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | ≤12 | NOT eligible | First trimester — SP contraindicated |
| 2 | 20 | Dose 1 | First eligible contact (if C1 was <13w) |
| 3 | 26 | Dose 2 | Minimum 1 month after Dose 1 |
| 4 | 30 | Dose 3 | Minimum 1 month after Dose 2 |
| 5 | 34 | Dose 4 | If ≥1 month since Dose 3 |
| 6 | 36 | Dose 5 | If ≥1 month since Dose 4 |
| 7 | 38 | — | Usually too close to Dose 5 |
| 8 | 40 | — | Near delivery |

### Late Booking IPTp-SP
- If first ANC visit is after 13 weeks: give first IPTp-SP dose immediately
- Catch up as many doses as possible with minimum 1-month intervals
- Example: First visit at 28 weeks → Dose 1 at 28w, Dose 2 at 32w, Dose 3 at 36w

### Contraindications
- **First trimester** (GA < 13 weeks) — teratogenic risk
- **HIV-positive women on daily cotrimoxazole** — increased adverse drug reactions
- **Known sulfa allergy**
- **Within 1 month of last SP dose**

## Insecticide-Treated Nets (ITNs)

- Provide at **first ANC contact** in endemic areas
- Counsel on nightly use throughout pregnancy and postpartum
- Replace if damaged or >3 years old (if not long-lasting type)
- Reinforce ITN use at every subsequent contact

## Case Management — Treatment by Trimester

| Trimester | Uncomplicated Malaria | Severe Malaria |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| First (< 13 weeks) | Quinine 10mg/kg TID × 7 days + Clindamycin 10mg/kg BID × 7 days | IV Artesunate 2.4mg/kg at 0, 12, 24h then daily + REFER |
| Second (13-27 weeks) | ACT (artemether-lumefantrine) × 3 days | IV Artesunate + REFER to higher facility |
| Third (28+ weeks) | ACT (artemether-lumefantrine) × 3 days | IV Artesunate + REFER to higher facility |

### Key Treatment Rules
- **NEVER use ACT in first trimester** (use quinine + clindamycin)
- ACT is safe and first-line in second and third trimesters
- Severe malaria at ANY GA: IV artesunate + immediate referral
- Monitor for anemia (malaria worsens pregnancy anemia)
- Recheck parasitemia at 48-72 hours post-treatment

## HIV-Positive Women in Malaria-Endemic Settings

Decision tree for malaria prevention in HIV-positive pregnant women:

```
IF HIV-positive AND on daily cotrimoxazole:
  → Do NOT give IPTp-SP (drug interaction)
  → Cotrimoxazole provides partial malaria protection
  → Still provide ITN
  → Still treat malaria episodes promptly

IF HIV-positive AND NOT on cotrimoxazole:
  → GIVE IPTp-SP as per standard schedule
  → Provide ITN
  → Standard case management
```

## Danger Signs — Malaria in Pregnancy

Refer IMMEDIATELY if:
- Temperature ≥ 39.5°C / 103°F
- Altered consciousness or convulsions (cerebral malaria)
- Severe anemia (Hb < 7 g/dL) with malaria
- Respiratory distress
- Jaundice
- Unable to eat or drink
- Very dark or reduced urine output
