# Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy — Classification & Management

## Classification Criteria

| Disorder | Systolic BP | Diastolic BP | Proteinuria | Onset | Other Features |
|----------|-----------|------------|-------------|-------|----------------|
| Chronic hypertension | ≥140 | ≥90 | May be present | Before 20 weeks or pre-existing | Known HTN prior to pregnancy |
| Gestational hypertension | ≥140 | ≥90 | Absent | After 20 weeks | New-onset, previously normotensive |
| Pre-eclampsia (without severe features) | ≥140 | ≥90 | ≥300mg/24h or ≥2+ dipstick | After 20 weeks | New-onset HTN + proteinuria |
| Pre-eclampsia (with severe features) | ≥160 | ≥110 | Present | After 20 weeks | PLUS any: headache, visual changes, epigastric pain, platelets <100k, creatinine >1.1, liver enzymes >2x, pulmonary edema |
| Superimposed pre-eclampsia | Worsening | Worsening | New or worsening | On top of chronic HTN | Chronic HTN + new proteinuria or sudden worsening |
| Eclampsia | Variable | Variable | Usually present | Any time | Seizures in a woman with pre-eclampsia features |

## Key Diagnostic Rules

- **Gestational HTN vs Pre-eclampsia**: The distinguishing factor is **proteinuria**. Elevated BP after 20 weeks WITHOUT proteinuria = gestational HTN. WITH proteinuria = pre-eclampsia.
- **Severe features**: BP ≥160/110 OR any end-organ dysfunction (headache, visual changes, epigastric/RUQ pain, pulmonary edema, low platelets, elevated creatinine, elevated liver enzymes)
- **Two readings required**: BP must be elevated on two readings at least 4 hours apart (or 15 minutes in urgent settings)
- **Chronic HTN**: diagnosed before 20 weeks or known pre-pregnancy. Does NOT resolve postpartum.

## Aspirin Prophylaxis Decision Tree

### HIGH Risk (≥1 factor → start aspirin)
- History of pre-eclampsia in prior pregnancy
- Chronic hypertension
- Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes
- Renal disease
- Autoimmune disease (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome)
- Multiple pregnancy (twins or higher)

### MODERATE Risk (≥2 factors → start aspirin)
- Nulliparity (first pregnancy)
- Age ≥ 35 years
- BMI > 30
- Family history of pre-eclampsia (mother or sister)
- Sociodemographic factors (African ancestry, low socioeconomic status)
- Previous pregnancy with adverse outcome (IUGR, stillbirth, placental abruption)
- Interpregnancy interval > 10 years

### Aspirin Regimen
- **Dose**: 75mg daily (some guidelines use 81mg or 150mg — 75mg is WHO standard)
- **Start**: Ideally at 12 weeks, but can start up to 20 weeks
- **Stop**: At 36 weeks (or at delivery if before 36 weeks)
- **Note**: Late initiation (after 16-20 weeks) has reduced but still some benefit

### Calcium Supplementation
- Recommended for ALL women at elevated pre-eclampsia risk
- **Dose**: 1.5-2g daily, divided into 3 doses
- Especially important in populations with low dietary calcium intake
- Take separately from iron supplements (≥2 hours apart)

## Management Pathways

### Gestational Hypertension (BP 140-159/90-109, no proteinuria, no severe features)
- Twice-weekly BP monitoring
- Weekly proteinuria screening
- Counsel on danger signs (headache, visual changes, epigastric pain)
- Fetal growth monitoring every 2-4 weeks
- Antihypertensive if BP consistently ≥150/100: methyldopa 250mg BID-TID or nifedipine 10mg BID
- Plan delivery at 37 weeks if stable
- Escalate if proteinuria develops (→ pre-eclampsia) or BP ≥160/110 (→ severe)

### Pre-eclampsia WITHOUT Severe Features
- Inpatient monitoring recommended where possible
- BP monitoring every 4-6 hours
- Daily proteinuria assessment
- Lab monitoring: platelets, creatinine, liver enzymes 2x/week
- Antihypertensive for BP ≥150/100
- Antenatal corticosteroids if GA 24-34 weeks (anticipating possible early delivery)
- Plan delivery at 37 weeks
- MgSO4 prophylaxis during labor and 24h postpartum

### Pre-eclampsia WITH Severe Features
- **EMERGENCY — IMMEDIATE STABILIZATION**
- Admit to high-level facility (CEmONC capable)
- MgSO4 loading dose IMMEDIATELY (see eclampsia protocol below)
- Antihypertensive for BP ≥160/110: IV hydralazine 5mg or nifedipine 10mg sublingual
- Target BP: 140-155/90-105 (avoid precipitous drops)
- Continuous fetal monitoring
- Antenatal corticosteroids if GA 24-34 weeks
- Delivery within 24-48 hours (or sooner if deteriorating)
- If GA < 34 weeks and stable: consider expectant management ONLY in tertiary facility

### Eclampsia — Emergency Protocol

**Phase 1: Immediate Response (0-5 minutes)**
1. Call for help — activate emergency response
2. Protect airway — left lateral position
3. Prevent injury — padded side rails, remove hazards
4. Do NOT insert anything in mouth
5. Time the seizure

**Phase 2: Magnesium Sulfate Loading (5-15 minutes)**
6. IV access — two large-bore cannulae
7. MgSO4 loading: 4g IV in 20% solution over 5-10 minutes
8. Followed by 5g IM in each buttock (10g total IM) if IV maintenance not available
9. OR: IV maintenance 1g/hour for 24 hours post last seizure

**Phase 3: Blood Pressure Control (concurrent)**
10. If BP ≥160/110: IV hydralazine 5mg slow push, repeat q20min (max 20mg)
11. OR nifedipine 10mg sublingual, repeat q30min (max 30mg)
12. Target: 140-155/90-105
13. Avoid precipitous drops (risk of placental hypoperfusion)

**Phase 4: Monitoring**
14. Continuous pulse oximetry
15. Urine output (Foley catheter) — aim >30mL/hour
16. Respiratory rate q1h (MgSO4 toxicity: RR <12)
17. Patellar reflexes q1h (absent = MgSO4 toxicity)
18. Have calcium gluconate 1g IV ready (MgSO4 antidote)

**Phase 5: Delivery Planning**
19. Eclampsia = indication for delivery regardless of GA
20. Stabilize FIRST — deliver within 12 hours of seizure
21. Cesarean if: not in labor, unfavorable cervix, fetal distress
22. Vaginal delivery acceptable if: in labor, favorable cervix, stable
23. Continue MgSO4 for 24 hours after last seizure or after delivery (whichever is later)

**Phase 6: Transfer (if needed)**
24. Stabilize before transfer
25. MgSO4 maintenance during transport
26. Qualified attendant with emergency kit during transfer
27. Receiving facility notified in advance

## Antihypertensive Quick Reference

| Drug | Dose | Route | Onset | Notes |
|------|------|-------|-------|-------|
| Methyldopa | 250-500mg BID-TID | Oral | 3-6 hours | First-line for chronic/gestational HTN |
| Nifedipine (long-acting) | 10-20mg BID | Oral | 30-60 min | Second-line oral |
| Hydralazine | 5mg, repeat q20min | IV | 10-20 min | Acute severe HTN |
| Nifedipine (immediate) | 10mg sublingual | Sublingual | 5-10 min | Acute severe HTN alternative |
| Labetalol | 20mg IV, escalate | IV | 5-10 min | If available; avoid in asthma |

## Red Flags — Escalation Triggers

Any of the following in a hypertensive patient require IMMEDIATE escalation:
- BP ≥160 systolic or ≥110 diastolic
- New or worsening proteinuria
- Headache not relieved by paracetamol
- Visual disturbances (blurring, scotomata, flashing lights)
- Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain
- Sudden swelling (face, hands)
- Decreased urine output
- Seizure (= eclampsia until proven otherwise)
- Abnormal fetal heart rate pattern
