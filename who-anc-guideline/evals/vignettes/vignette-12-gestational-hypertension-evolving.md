## Expected Evaluation Points

### PRIMARY CLASSIFICATION: GESTATIONAL HYPERTENSION
- **Diagnostic criteria met:**
  - New-onset hypertension: BP >= 140/90 mmHg (148/95, confirmed on repeat reading at 146/94)
  - After 20 weeks gestation (first elevated at 32 weeks; all prior contacts normotensive)
  - NO proteinuria (urine dipstick protein negative)
  - NO severe features (no headache, no visual changes, no epigastric pain)
- **This is NOT pre-eclampsia:** The critical discriminating factor is the ABSENCE of proteinuria and the ABSENCE of severe features. Pre-eclampsia requires hypertension PLUS proteinuria and/or end-organ dysfunction. This patient has hypertension only.
- **This is NOT chronic hypertension:** All BP readings before 20 weeks were normal (112/68 at 12 weeks, 118/72 at 20 weeks)

### MANAGEMENT PLAN:
1. **Blood pressure monitoring:** Twice-weekly BP checks — patient should return to the facility every 3-4 days for BP measurement, or more frequently if any new symptoms develop
2. **Proteinuria screening:** Weekly urine dipstick for protein — the key concern is PROGRESSION to pre-eclampsia; serial protein monitoring is essential
3. **Antihypertensive medication:** Consider initiating oral methyldopa (250 mg two to three times daily) to maintain BP <140/90 — decision per clinical officer/physician
4. **Continue aspirin:** Low-dose aspirin 75 mg daily should be continued
5. **Continue calcium:** 1.5 g daily in divided doses
6. **Danger sign counseling — CRITICAL at this visit:**
   - Instruct patient on warning signs that indicate PROGRESSION to pre-eclampsia:
     - Severe or persistent headache (especially frontal)
     - Visual changes (blurred vision, seeing spots, flashing lights)
     - Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain
     - Sudden swelling of face or hands
     - Decreased fetal movement
   - Emphasize: If ANY of these symptoms develop, present IMMEDIATELY to the nearest facility — do not wait for the next scheduled appointment
7. **Delivery planning:** Plan delivery at 37 weeks if gestational hypertension remains stable without progression to pre-eclampsia. If BP becomes difficult to control or pre-eclampsia features develop, earlier delivery may be indicated.
8. **Facility delivery:** Must deliver at a facility with capacity for emergency cesarean section and management of hypertensive emergencies
9. **Laboratory:** Consider baseline blood work (CBC with platelets, LFTs, creatinine) to have comparison values if condition progresses
10. **Fetal surveillance:** Monitor fetal growth — consider growth ultrasound at 34-36 weeks given hypertension
