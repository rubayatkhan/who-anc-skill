## Assessment

### 1. Chronic Hypertension in Pregnancy
Grace has pre-existing chronic hypertension managed with methyldopa. Today's BP of 135/88 mmHg is elevated but not at severe levels (below 160/110 threshold). There is no proteinuria and no symptoms suggesting pre-eclampsia at this time. Current antihypertensive should be continued — methyldopa is safe in pregnancy. ACE inhibitors and ARBs must be avoided.

### 2. Pre-eclampsia Risk Assessment
This patient has multiple risk factors for developing pre-eclampsia:
- Chronic hypertension (major risk factor)
- Nulliparity (first pregnancy)
- Advanced maternal age (38 years, above 35 threshold)
- Obesity (BMI 33.1, above 30)
- Family history of pre-eclampsia (mother)

Based on WHO recommendations, she is classified as **high risk for pre-eclampsia**. Prophylactic measures are indicated.

### 3. Obesity and GDM Risk
With a BMI of 33.1 and family history of type 2 diabetes, Grace is at increased risk for gestational diabetes. Current random glucose is normal (5.8 mmol/L), but formal screening with OGTT should be performed at 24-28 weeks. Consider earlier screening given the risk factors.

## Plan

### Medications
1. **Continue methyldopa 250mg twice daily** for chronic hypertension. Review dose if BP remains above 140/90 at next visit.
2. **Start low-dose aspirin 75mg daily** for pre-eclampsia prophylaxis. Ideally should have started at 12 weeks, but starting now at 14 weeks is still beneficial. Continue until approximately 36 weeks.
3. **Start calcium supplementation 1.5-2g daily** in divided doses for pre-eclampsia prevention, given the high-risk status and typical low dietary calcium intake in the region.
4. **Iron supplementation:** Standard prophylactic dose (intermittent or daily depending on local protocol). Hemoglobin is normal.
5. **Folic acid 400mcg daily** throughout pregnancy.

### Monitoring
- Blood pressure to be checked at every ANC visit. Given the high-risk profile, more frequent visits than standard schedule.
- Urine dipstick for proteinuria at each visit to monitor for superimposed pre-eclampsia.
- Monitor for danger signs: severe headache, visual changes, epigastric pain, sudden swelling.
- Home BP monitoring encouraged — continue keeping a log.
- Repeat hemoglobin at 28-30 weeks.

### Referral and Planning
- Discuss with obstetrician regarding management plan given high-risk status.
- Plan delivery at facility with emergency obstetric care capacity (C-section, magnesium sulfate availability, blood bank).
- GDM screening at 24-28 weeks with OGTT.

### Counseling
- Pre-eclampsia warning signs and when to present urgently
- Importance of medication adherence (methyldopa, aspirin, calcium)
- Appropriate weight gain targets for obese BMI category
- Balanced nutrition, limiting excessive salt intake
- Anxiety support — reassure about current stable findings, offer referral if needed
- Birth preparedness planning

### Next Appointment
- Return in approximately 6 weeks (at ~20 weeks gestation) for second contact
- Ultrasound anatomy scan at that visit
- Continue aspirin, calcium, methyldopa, iron, and folic acid
