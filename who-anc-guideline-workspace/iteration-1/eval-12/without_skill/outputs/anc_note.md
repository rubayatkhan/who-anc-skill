## 2. Risk Classification

### Primary Classification: GESTATIONAL HYPERTENSION

**Diagnostic reasoning:**

- **New-onset hypertension after 20 weeks gestation:** BP of 148/95 mmHg confirmed on repeat (146/94 mmHg), both readings meeting the threshold of >= 140/90 mmHg. This is the first episode of elevated BP, arising at 32 weeks.
- **Previously normotensive:** All documented BP readings before 20 weeks were normal (112/68 at 12 weeks, 118/72 at 20 weeks), excluding chronic hypertension.
- **No proteinuria:** Urine dipstick is negative for protein.
- **No severe features:** No headache, no visual disturbances, no epigastric or right upper quadrant pain, no nausea/vomiting, no facial swelling.

**This is NOT pre-eclampsia** because proteinuria is absent and there are no signs of end-organ dysfunction. Pre-eclampsia requires hypertension PLUS proteinuria and/or evidence of end-organ involvement.

**This is NOT chronic hypertension** because all blood pressure readings documented before 20 weeks gestation were within normal limits.

**This is NOT a hypertensive emergency** because:
- Systolic BP is < 160 mmHg and diastolic BP is < 110 mmHg
- No severe symptoms are present (headache, visual changes, epigastric pain)
- Magnesium sulfate and emergency delivery are NOT indicated

### Additional Risk Factors
- **Nulliparity:** First pregnancy, which is an independent risk factor for pre-eclampsia. This was appropriately identified at booking and low-dose aspirin was initiated.
- **Malaria exposure:** Resides in malaria-endemic area (Dar es Salaam). Ongoing IPTp-SP and ITN use are essential.

### Conditions Excluded or Not Present
- Chronic hypertension: excluded (normal BP prior to 20 weeks)
- Pre-eclampsia: not currently present (no proteinuria, no severe features)
- Gestational diabetes: screened negative at Contact 3
- Anemia: Hb 11.5 g/dL at Contact 4 (normal)

## 4. Recommended Interventions

### A. Blood Pressure Management

1. **Initiate antihypertensive therapy:** Start oral methyldopa 250 mg two to three times daily with the goal of maintaining BP below 140/90 mmHg. Methyldopa is the preferred first-line agent in pregnancy and is available at this facility. Titrate dose as needed under clinical officer/physician guidance.

2. **Increased BP monitoring:** Schedule twice-weekly BP checks. Patient should return to the facility every 3-4 days for blood pressure measurement. If symptoms develop or BP worsens, she should present immediately without waiting for the next scheduled visit.

3. **Serial proteinuria screening:** Perform weekly urine dipstick for protein. The primary concern is progression from gestational hypertension to pre-eclampsia. Serial monitoring is essential to detect this transition early.

### B. Laboratory Investigations

4. **Baseline blood work:** Order complete blood count with platelet count, liver function tests (AST/ALT), and serum creatinine. These values establish a baseline for comparison should the condition progress toward pre-eclampsia or HELLP syndrome.

### C. Fetal Surveillance

5. **Growth ultrasound:** Schedule a fetal growth ultrasound at 34-36 weeks to assess for intrauterine growth restriction, which is a recognized complication of hypertensive disorders in pregnancy.

6. **Ongoing fetal monitoring:** Continue monitoring fetal heart rate and fetal movements at each visit. Instruct the patient to perform daily fetal kick counts and report any decrease in movement.

### D. Delivery Planning

7. **Planned delivery at 37 weeks:** If gestational hypertension remains stable without progression to pre-eclampsia, plan delivery at approximately 37 weeks gestation. Earlier delivery may be warranted if BP becomes difficult to control, pre-eclampsia develops, or fetal compromise is identified.

8. **Facility delivery:** Delivery must occur at a facility equipped for emergency cesarean section and management of hypertensive emergencies. Arrange referral to the regional referral hospital (8 km away) for delivery, as it has comprehensive emergency obstetric care capacity.

## 9. Danger Sign Counseling -- CRITICAL AT THIS VISIT

Given the new diagnosis of gestational hypertension, thorough danger sign counseling is of paramount importance. The patient must understand warning signs indicating possible progression to pre-eclampsia:

### Signs Requiring IMMEDIATE Presentation to a Health Facility:
1. **Severe or persistent headache** -- especially frontal headache that does not respond to rest or simple analgesia
2. **Visual disturbances** -- blurred vision, seeing spots or flashing lights, temporary loss of vision
3. **Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain** -- pain in the upper abdomen or below the ribs on the right side, described as a "band-like" tightness
4. **Sudden swelling of the face or hands** -- especially if rapid in onset (distinguish from the gradual, symmetric ankle edema she is currently experiencing)
5. **Decreased or absent fetal movement** -- any significant change in the baby's usual movement pattern
6. **Severe nausea or vomiting** -- especially in the third trimester
7. **Any vaginal bleeding**
8. **Difficulty breathing or chest pain**

### Key Messages:
- **Do NOT wait for the next scheduled appointment** if any of these symptoms develop. Present to the nearest health facility immediately, at any time of day or night.
- **Bring her ANC card** and medication list to any unscheduled visit.
- **Inform her partner/family** about these warning signs so they can assist in recognizing them and facilitating transport if needed.
- Provide the patient with clear instructions on how to reach the referral hospital (8 km away) in case of emergency, including transportation options.

## 10. Additional Counseling Topics

### Blood Pressure-Related
- Explain what gestational hypertension means in plain language: her blood pressure is higher than normal for the first time during this pregnancy, and while this is manageable, it needs close monitoring.
- Explain the importance of attending all scheduled BP checks and not missing appointments.
- Discuss the medication (methyldopa): take as prescribed, do not skip doses, may cause mild drowsiness initially.
- Rest when possible: avoid prolonged standing, elevate legs when seated.
- Continue normal activities including work but listen to her body and rest when fatigued.

### Nutrition
- Continue balanced diet with adequate protein, fruits, and vegetables.
- Continue calcium-rich foods (dairy, beans, small fish with bones) in addition to calcium supplementation.
- Reduce salt intake to a moderate level (do not add excessive salt to food), though complete salt restriction is not recommended.
- Adequate hydration.

### Birth Preparedness and Complication Readiness
- Given the new hypertension diagnosis, birth preparedness planning takes on added urgency:
  - Identify the delivery facility (referral hospital recommended)
  - Arrange transportation in advance, including for emergencies
  - Identify a blood donor if possible
  - Save funds for potential emergency costs
  - Identify a decision-maker in her absence
- Discuss the plan for delivery at 37 weeks if the condition remains stable.

### Breastfeeding
- Early initiation of breastfeeding within 1 hour of delivery
- Exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months
- Methyldopa is compatible with breastfeeding

### Postpartum Follow-Up
- Blood pressure monitoring must continue postpartum, as gestational hypertension may persist or worsen in the early postpartum period.
- Schedule postpartum BP check within 1 week of delivery.
